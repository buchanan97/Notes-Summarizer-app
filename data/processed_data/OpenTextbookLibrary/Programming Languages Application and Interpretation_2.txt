We will include mistakes, not because I don t know the answer, but because this is the best way for you to learn . Including mistakes makes it impossible for you to read passively: you must instead engage with the material, because you can never be sure of the veracity of what you re reading. At the end, you ll always get to the right answer. However, this non-linear path is more frustrating in the short term (you will often be tempted to say, Just tell me the answer, already! ), and it makes the book a poor reference guide (you can t open up to a random page and be sure what it says is correct). However, that feeling of frustration is the sensation of learning. I don t know of a way around it. At various points you will encounter this: Exercise This is an exercise. Do try it. This is a traditional textbook exercise. It s something you need to do on your own. If you re using this book as part of a course, this may very well have been assigned as homework. In contrast, you will also nd exercise-like questions that look like this: Do Now! There s an activity here! Do you see it? When you get to one of these, stop.
